Task 1 — Giving Advice

A friend or acquaintance has a problem and needs your guidance. Offer thoughtful, structured advice drawing on empathy and practical reasoning.

⏱ Prep: 30 sec 🎙 Speak: 90 sec
◆ CLB 10+ Strategy: Acknowledge the person's feelings first, then give 2–3 concrete pieces of advice with reasoning. Use hedged, nuanced language (rather than commands). Close with an encouraging offer of continued support. Aim for moderately demanding non-routine register throughout.
Common Scenario Types
OpeningAcknowledge + Frame (5–10 sec)

I completely understand how stressful this situation must be for you, and I really appreciate you reaching out. Based on what you've shared, I have a few thoughts that I hope will be helpful as you navigate this.

Alternative Openings
That sounds like a genuinely difficult position to be in, and I think there are some concrete steps you can take to improve the situation.
I can see why you're feeling conflicted about this — it's not a straightforward decision, but let me share what I would consider if I were in your shoes.
Middle2–3 Pieces of Advice with Reasons (60–70 sec)

First and foremost, I'd suggest [Advice #1 — e.g., having an honest conversation with your manager about your concerns]. The reason I recommend this is that [Reason — e.g., unaddressed frustrations tend to compound over time, and managers often appreciate directness more than people expect]. You might approach it by [specific tactic — e.g., requesting a private meeting and framing it around your professional growth].

Beyond that, it would also be worth [Advice #2 — e.g., updating your resume and quietly exploring other opportunities]. This isn't about being disloyal — it's about giving yourself options and a clearer sense of your own market value. Even if you ultimately decide to stay, knowing what's out there can be quite empowering.

Finally, I'd encourage you to [Advice #3 — e.g., set yourself a clear timeline — say, three months — and then reassess how things have changed]. Having a concrete benchmark will prevent the situation from dragging on indefinitely, which can really affect your morale and productivity.

Advice Connector Phrases
One thing I would prioritize is... / What I think matters most here is...
Another avenue worth considering is... / On top of that, it might also help to...
The reason I say this is... / My thinking behind that is... / This is important because...
ClosingEncourage + Offer Support (10 sec)

I know none of this is easy, but I genuinely believe you have the resilience and skills to come through this stronger. Please don't hesitate to reach out if you want to talk things through further — I'm always happy to help.

Alternative Closings
Whatever you decide, just know that you don't have to figure this out alone. I'm rooting for you.
I hope some of that is useful. Trust your instincts — you know the situation better than anyone.
OpeningAcknowledge + Empathize

That sounds like a really emotionally draining situation, and I want you to know that your feelings are completely valid. Let me share a few thoughts that might help you find some clarity.

MiddleAdvice Development

The first thing I'd suggest is [e.g., giving yourself some space to reflect before making any major decisions]. When emotions are running high, it's easy to say or do things we later regret, so a bit of distance can actually be incredibly clarifying.

I'd also recommend [e.g., having an open and honest conversation — using "I feel" statements rather than accusations]. This approach tends to lower defensiveness and opens the door to genuine dialogue. If direct conversation feels too charged, [e.g., writing a letter or seeking the help of a counsellor] can be a constructive alternative.

Ultimately, what matters most is [e.g., your own well-being and sense of self-respect]. Whatever path you choose, make sure it's one you can feel at peace with.

Closing

Relationships are complicated, and there are no perfect answers here. But I think you already have a good sense of what you need — sometimes we just need someone else to remind us to trust that instinct. I'm here for you.

Opening

Financial and housing decisions can feel overwhelming, especially when there's a lot at stake. I have some thoughts that might help you weigh your options more clearly.

Middle

My first piece of advice would be to [e.g., sit down and map out your complete financial picture — income, expenses, savings, and any upcoming costs]. It sounds basic, but having everything written out makes it much easier to identify what's actually feasible.

From there, I'd strongly recommend [e.g., speaking with a financial advisor or housing counsellor before committing to anything]. Many of these services are free or low-cost, and a professional perspective can help you avoid costly mistakes that might not be obvious on the surface.

It might also be worth [e.g., exploring government programs or community resources designed to support people in your situation]. You might be surprised by the options available — many people simply aren't aware of them.

Closing

These situations can feel isolating, but you're asking the right questions. Take it one step at a time, and don't be afraid to ask for help — that's what it's there for. I'm happy to help you research any of these options if that would be useful.

Opening

I really appreciate you sharing something so personal. Health and lifestyle changes can be challenging, but the fact that you're thinking about this means you're already moving in the right direction.

Middle

The first thing I'd encourage is [e.g., consulting a doctor or healthcare professional before making any major changes]. Everyone's body and circumstances are different, and professional guidance ensures that what you're doing is both safe and effective for you specifically.

I'd also suggest [e.g., starting small — focusing on one sustainable change rather than overhauling everything at once]. Research consistently shows that gradual adjustments are far more likely to stick than drastic overnight transformations.

Additionally, it can really help to [e.g., build in accountability — whether through a friend, an app, or a community group]. Having external motivation during the early stages makes a significant difference in maintaining momentum.

Closing

Progress isn't always linear, so please be patient with yourself. The important thing is consistency over time, not perfection every day. You've got this, and I'm cheering you on.

Opening

Moving is one of the most exciting yet stressful things a person can do, so I completely understand why you're feeling pulled in different directions. Let me share some thoughts that might make the decision — and the process — a little easier.

Middle

Before anything else, I'd recommend [e.g., making a detailed list of your priorities — proximity to work, school quality, cost of living, community — and then researching how different areas stack up]. This transforms an abstract decision into a concrete comparison.

I'd also suggest [e.g., visiting the area if at all possible, even for a weekend, before committing]. Reading about a place and actually experiencing it are very different things, and first-hand impressions matter enormously.

Practically speaking, it would be wise to [e.g., give yourself at least two months for the move itself, and start decluttering early]. Most people underestimate how much time and energy the logistics require.

Closing

Change can be daunting, but it can also be an incredible opportunity. I think you're going to handle this beautifully. Let me know if there's anything specific I can help you look into.

Task 2 — Talking about a Personal Experience

Describe a memorable personal experience related to a given theme. Use vivid detail, narrative structure, and reflective commentary.

⏱ Prep: 30 sec 🎙 Speak: 60 sec
◆ CLB 10+ Strategy: Use a clear narrative arc: Set the scene → develop the story with concrete and abstract details → reflect on the meaning/impact. Use vivid, varied vocabulary and figures of speech. Avoid listing events; create a story with emotional resonance.
Common Theme Types
OpeningSet the Scene (10 sec)

One experience that stands out in my memory is [event/achievement]. It happened about [timeframe], and at the time, I had no idea how much it would shape the way I see [relevant theme].

Alternatives
If I think about a moment that genuinely tested my limits, it would have to be the time I... [situation]
There's an experience from [timeframe] that I still reflect on quite often — it was the period when I had to...
MiddleDevelop the Story with Detail (35–40 sec)

At the time, I was [context — role, location, circumstances]. The challenge I was facing was [specific problem/goal], and what made it particularly daunting was [complicating factor — time pressure, lack of resources, emotional difficulty, etc.].

I remember feeling [emotion — overwhelmed / uncertain / determined], but I decided to [key action you took]. Looking back, that decision turned out to be [pivotal / more difficult than I expected / surprisingly effective] because [reason/consequence].

Detail-Building Phrases
What struck me most was the realization that... / What I hadn't anticipated was...
Despite the initial setbacks, I managed to... / Against all odds, we were able to...
The turning point came when... / Everything changed the moment...
ClosingReflect on the Meaning (10 sec)

That experience taught me [key lesson or insight]. To this day, it informs the way I approach [relevant aspect of life/work], and I genuinely believe I came out of it a more [resilient / patient / perceptive] person.

Alternative Closings
It wasn't easy, but I wouldn't trade that experience — it gave me a depth of understanding I couldn't have gained any other way.
Looking back, I realize that the hardest moments often turn into the most formative ones.
Opening

One trip that left a lasting impression on me was when I visited [place]. I'd been looking forward to it for a long time, but nothing quite prepared me for what it was actually like.

Middle

I arrived in [place] during [season/time], and the first thing that struck me was [vivid sensory detail — the landscape, the sounds, the energy of the place]. What I hadn't expected was [surprising element — cultural difference, unexpected beauty, a challenge you encountered].

One particular moment that encapsulates the whole experience was when [specific memorable event — a conversation, a sight, a moment of getting lost, etc.]. It made me realize [insight about culture / people / yourself].

Closing

That journey broadened my perspective in ways I hadn't anticipated. Experiencing a different way of life firsthand really challenges your assumptions and leaves you with a deeper appreciation for both the world and where you come from.

Opening

One of the most rewarding experiences I've had was learning [skill/subject]. It was something I'd been meaning to try for years, and when I finally took the plunge, the journey turned out to be far more interesting than the destination.

Middle

When I first started, I was genuinely out of my depth. [Describe your early struggles with specific detail]. I remember feeling a mixture of frustration and fascination — frustrated because [reason], but fascinated because [what kept drawing you in].

Over time, I developed a routine of [how you practiced/learned], and gradually the pieces started to fall into place. The moment I realized I'd genuinely made progress was when [specific milestone moment].

Closing

That experience reinforced something I now firmly believe: the process of learning something from scratch — with all the awkwardness and uncertainty it entails — is one of the most humbling and ultimately fulfilling things you can do for yourself.

Opening

I can think of one particular situation that caught me completely off guard — it was the kind of event you simply can't plan for, and it ended up becoming one of the most memorable experiences of my life.

Middle

It happened when [describe the unexpected situation with context]. My immediate reaction was [emotional response — disbelief, alarm, laughter], and in that moment, I had to [decision/action you took].

What unfolded next was [how events played out]. Looking back, I'm struck by how differently things could have gone if [counterfactual — I had hesitated / made a different choice / not had the help I did].

Closing

That day reminded me that life rarely goes according to plan — and that sometimes the unscripted moments are the ones that teach you the most about yourself and the people around you.

Task 3 — Describing a Scene

Describe what you see in an image in rich, organized detail. Cover the setting, people, actions, atmosphere, and any notable elements.

⏱ Prep: 30 sec 🎙 Speak: 60 sec
◆ CLB 10+ Strategy: Move from general → specific. Describe: overall setting → foreground details → background → atmosphere/mood → any implied story. Use precise, descriptive vocabulary. Avoid simple listing — narrate with connecting language and inference.
Common Scene Types
OpeningEstablish the Overall Scene (8–10 sec)

The image depicts what appears to be [general setting — a busy urban park / a quiet suburban street / a crowded market square]. The overall atmosphere is [lively and energetic / calm and peaceful / bustling and chaotic].

Alternative Openers
What I see here is a scene set in [location], captured on what looks like a [season/time of day].
This image shows an outdoor [setting] that gives off a distinctly [adjective] atmosphere.
MiddleForeground → Background → Details (35–40 sec)

In the foreground, I can see [main subjects — people, objects, activities]. They appear to be [what they're doing — e.g., engaged in conversation / walking briskly / gathered around something], which suggests [implied context — they may be friends meeting up / commuters on their way to work].

Moving further into the scene, the background reveals [distant elements — buildings, trees, vehicles, other people]. The [specific detail — colour of the sky / style of the architecture / condition of the path] gives the scene a distinctly [adjective — nostalgic / contemporary / vibrant] quality.

What I find particularly striking about this image is [a notable or interesting element — a contrast, a focal point, an implied story].

ClosingOverall Impression / Inference (10 sec)

Overall, the scene conveys a sense of [mood — everyday community life / leisure and connection / urban momentum]. If I had to guess, this might have been taken during [time/occasion/context], and it captures the [essence / spirit / texture] of that environment beautifully.

Opening

The scene appears to take place in a [office / conference room / open-plan workspace]. The setting looks [professional and organized / collaborative and informal / focused and task-oriented].

Middle

In the foreground, there [is a person / are several people] who appear to be [typing at a computer / reviewing documents together / presenting at a whiteboard]. Their body language suggests [concentration / collaboration / a discussion in progress].

The workspace itself features [notable office elements — monitors, plants, whiteboards, filing cabinets]. The [lighting / layout / level of activity] gives the impression that this is a [busy deadline environment / casual team culture / formal corporate setting].

Closing

The overall impression is one of [productivity / collaboration / focused individual work]. It's the kind of environment that seems [conducive to creativity / structured for efficiency / built around teamwork].

Opening

The image appears to show a [domestic interior / social gathering / family event] taking place in what looks like [a living room / a kitchen / a dining space]. The mood is unmistakably [warm and convivial / relaxed and casual / festive].

Middle

The main focus of the scene is [group of people / a person performing a domestic activity]. They appear to be [sharing a meal / celebrating an occasion / engaged in conversation], and there's a palpable sense of [ease and familiarity / joy and connection] in their interactions.

The surroundings are [homey and well-lived-in / neatly decorated / festively arranged], with [specific details — family photos on the wall / seasonal decorations / food spread on the table] that add depth to the narrative of the scene.

Closing

The scene evokes a strong sense of [community / belonging / the simple pleasures of everyday life]. It's the sort of image that feels both intimate and universally relatable.

Opening

The image shows what appears to be [a grocery store / a shopping mall / a market / a retail shop]. The environment looks [busy and well-stocked / bright and commercial / crowded with shoppers].

Middle

In the foreground, [a shopper / a staff member / a group of people] can be seen [browsing the aisles / assisting a customer / making a purchase]. The products on display include [visible items], arranged in a way that seems [deliberate and promotional / practical and functional].

What draws my attention is [a particular element — a promotional display / the lighting / how the space is organized], which tells me something about [the store's brand / the customer experience they're aiming for].

Closing

Overall, the scene captures the familiar rhythm of [consumer life / daily errands / community commerce] — nothing extraordinary on the surface, yet full of small human moments if you look closely.

Task 4 — Making Predictions

Look at an image and predict what might happen next. Offer 2–3 plausible outcomes, linking them logically to what you observe in the scene.

⏱ Prep: 30 sec 🎙 Speak: 60 sec
◆ CLB 10+ Strategy: Use hedged prediction language ("It's quite likely that...", "I'd expect..."). Root each prediction in visible evidence from the image. Offer a primary prediction with reasoning, then one or two alternatives. Show sophisticated cause-and-effect thinking.
OpeningBriefly anchor to what you see (8 sec)

Based on what I can see in this image — [quick description of the key elements] — I can make a few reasonable predictions about what might unfold next.

Alternatives
Looking at this scene, there are a number of directions things could go, depending on several factors I can identify in the image.
The situation depicted here has a few telling signs that help me anticipate what comes next.
MiddlePrimary + Secondary Predictions with Evidence (40 sec)

My primary prediction would be that [most likely outcome]. This seems probable because [evidence from the image — body language, objects, spatial arrangement, expressions]. Given that [contextual clue], I'd expect this to result in [consequence or next step].

Alternatively, it's also possible that [secondary scenario]. This might happen if [condition or factor that could shift the outcome]. The [specific detail — e.g., the tension in their posture / the direction they're facing] suggests that this outcome, while less likely, can't be ruled out.

In the best case scenario, [optimistic prediction], which would suggest [positive resolution].

Prediction Language (CLB 10+)
It's quite likely that... / I would expect... / There's a reasonable chance that...
Based on [evidence], my best guess would be... / The indicators suggest...
If things unfold as the image implies... / Assuming this trajectory continues...
The most plausible outcome, in my view, is... / What I'd anticipate is...
ClosingAcknowledge uncertainty / wrap up (10 sec)

Of course, predicting the future from a single snapshot is inherently limited, but the visual cues available in this image make [primary prediction] the most plausible conclusion. Whatever happens, the scene is clearly building toward some kind of [resolution / confrontation / transition].

Alternative Closings
While there's always an element of uncertainty, the weight of evidence points toward [primary prediction].
Ultimately, it depends on how [key person/factor] responds — but I'd lean strongly toward [outcome].
Hedged Prediction Phrases for Task 4

I would anticipate that...
The evidence suggests...
In all probability...
There's a distinct possibility that...
If I had to make a prediction...
This could well lead to...
The most plausible outcome is...
Task 5 — Comparing & Persuading

Compare two options shown in images and persuade a specific person to choose one, tailoring your reasoning to their known needs and concerns.

⏱ Prep: 60 sec 🎙 Speak: 60 sec
◆ CLB 10+ Strategy: Tailor your argument directly to the specific person and their needs. Acknowledge the strengths of Option A briefly, then make a clear, persuasive case for Option B (or whichever you choose). Use comparative structures fluently. Close with a decisive recommendation.
Common Comparison Types
OpeningAcknowledge the choice + Signal your recommendation (8 sec)

I've looked at both options carefully, and taking into account everything you've told me about [the person's priorities — your budget / your schedule / what matters most to you], I'm firmly recommending [Option A or B]. Here's my thinking.

MiddleComparative Argument (40 sec)

Now, I understand the appeal of [the other option] — particularly [its strongest selling point, e.g., the lower cost / the central location / the modern design]. That said, when you weigh it against what you actually need, I think it falls short in a few important ways.

[Your recommended option], on the other hand, offers [key advantage #1 — e.g., significantly more space / a much better value for money / easier access to transit], which is directly relevant to [the person's specific situation]. Beyond that, it also provides [advantage #2], and given that [reason tied to their needs], this really tips the scales in its favour.

What also gives me confidence in this recommendation is [a third supporting point or a counter to the other option's drawback].

Comparison Connectors
While [Option A] has its merits, [Option B] is clearly superior in terms of...
Compared to [Option A], [Option B] offers a much more [adjective] solution for your specific situation.
The key differentiator here is... / What ultimately sets [Option B] apart is...
ClosingClear, decisive close (10 sec)

For all those reasons, I'm confident that [Option B] is the right call for you. You might have a moment of doubt when you see [the other option's appeal], but I genuinely believe that once you [experience it / make the move / see the results], you won't look back.

Alternative Closings
Trust me on this one — [Option B] is far better suited to where you are and where you want to be.
The choice ultimately comes down to [key factor], and on that measure, [Option B] wins without question.
Opening

Between these two options, I think the choice is actually clearer than it might first appear — especially given your specific situation. I'd go with [product/item B], and here's why.

Middle

The [other option] might seem attractive because of [its price / its appearance / its brand recognition], but when you look closely, [it has a notable limitation — less durable / fewer features / not well-suited to your use case].

My recommendation — [Option B] — addresses exactly what you need: [key benefit #1], which will matter a great deal when [practical situation]. It also [benefit #2], which means over time, you'll likely find it [more cost-effective / more enjoyable / more reliable].

Closing

I know it might feel like the bigger investment upfront, but the long-term value is genuinely there. Go with [Option B] — I think you'll be very glad you did.

Opening

After thinking through both plans carefully, I think [Plan B] is significantly better suited to what you're trying to accomplish — and I'd like to walk you through my reasoning.

Middle

[Plan A] has a certain appeal in that [its strength], but the challenge is that [its limitation or trade-off]. Given [the person's constraints — time, budget, goals], that's a meaningful drawback.

[Plan B], however, aligns much better with what you need because [reason 1] and [reason 2]. Perhaps most importantly, it allows you to [key outcome relevant to the person's goal], which I know is a priority for you.

Closing

At the end of the day, both plans have their merits, but [Plan B] is simply the stronger fit for your circumstances. I'm confident you'll feel the difference quickly.

Task 6 — Dealing with a Difficult Situation

You must leave a voicemail or spoken message to resolve a difficult or uncomfortable situation. Be direct, diplomatic, and solution-focused.

⏱ Prep: 60 sec 🎙 Speak: 60 sec
◆ CLB 10+ Strategy: Sound professional and calm even when discussing a problem. Identify yourself, state the situation clearly, propose a specific solution, and request follow-up. Avoid being aggressive or vague. Maintain a formal-to-semi-formal tone as a voicemail message.
Common Situation Types
OpeningIdentify Yourself + State Purpose (10 sec)

Hello, my name is [Your Name], and I'm calling regarding [specific issue — an order I placed / a service I received / a recent visit to your location]. I want to bring a concern to your attention, and I'm hoping we can resolve it together.

MiddleDescribe the Problem + Propose a Solution (35–40 sec)

On [date or timeframe], I [describe the specific issue clearly and factually — received a damaged item / was overcharged / experienced poor service]. I was [quite surprised / genuinely disappointed / frustrated], especially given that I've been a loyal customer for [duration].

I've already [any steps already taken — checked the receipt / reviewed the terms of service / reached out via email], and the issue remains unresolved. What I'd like to request is [specific resolution — a full refund / a replacement / a callback from your customer service manager]. I think that would be a fair and reasonable outcome under the circumstances.

My reference number, if relevant, is [number], and you can reach me at [phone number] or via email at [address].

Tone Calibration Phrases
I'm not looking to cause any inconvenience — I simply want to... / I'm sure this is something we can sort out easily.
I understand these things happen, but I do think it warrants attention because...
I'd appreciate a prompt response, as this has already caused some disruption to...
ClosingRequest Callback + Thank (10 sec)

I'd appreciate a callback at your earliest convenience — ideally by [date]. Thank you for taking the time to listen to this message, and I look forward to hearing from you.

Opening

Hi [Name], it's [Your Name]. I wanted to reach out right away because something has come up and I owe you both an explanation and an apology.

Middle

Unfortunately, [describe the situation honestly — I wasn't able to make it / I made an error that affected you / something unexpected came up]. I completely understand if this has caused inconvenience, and I take full responsibility for [your part in the situation].

To make things right, I'd like to [proposed remedy — reschedule at a time that works for you / cover any costs this has caused / find another way to address the situation]. Please know that this was not intentional, and I genuinely value [our working relationship / your trust / the opportunity to make this right].

Closing

Please give me a call when you have a moment — I'd like to speak with you directly and sort this out properly. Again, I'm truly sorry for any disruption this may have caused.

Opening

Good day, my name is [Name], and I'm contacting you regarding a request for [accommodation / adjustment / exception] related to [context — my tenancy / my workplace / my enrollment].

Middle

The situation I find myself in is [explain the circumstances clearly and without over-sharing]. Due to [reason — a medical issue / a family commitment / a financial change], I'm hoping you might be able to [specific request — extend a deadline / adjust payment terms / grant a temporary exception].

I want to assure you that this is a temporary situation, and I'm committed to [demonstrating good faith — fulfilling my obligations / making up any missed responsibilities]. I have [any supporting documentation available if needed].

Closing

I'd be grateful if you could review this request and get back to me with your thoughts. I'm reachable at [contact info]. Thank you very much for your time and consideration.

Opening

Hello, my name is [Name] — I live at [unit/address]. I'm calling because there's a situation that's been affecting our community, and I'd like to address it respectfully.

Middle

Over the past [timeframe], I've noticed [describe the issue — excessive noise / parking obstruction / waste disposal problems]. While I recognize that [person] may not be aware of the impact this is having, it has been causing [describe the effect — disruption to sleep / safety concerns / inconvenience for other residents].

I'm not looking to escalate things unnecessarily — I genuinely believe this can be resolved with a simple conversation. What I'd kindly request is [specific behaviour change or accommodation]. I'm very happy to meet and discuss this in person if that's preferable.

Closing

I appreciate you taking the time to listen, and I look forward to finding a solution that works for everyone. Please feel free to reach me at [contact]. Thank you.

Task 7 — Expressing Opinions

Share and defend your perspective on a social, community, or policy-related topic. Demonstrate nuanced reasoning and awareness of counterarguments.

⏱ Prep: 30 sec 🎙 Speak: 90 sec
◆ CLB 10+ Strategy: State your position clearly upfront. Develop 2–3 points with concrete reasoning, examples, or comparisons. Acknowledge the other side briefly to demonstrate balanced, sophisticated thinking. Conclude with a strong restatement of your position.
Common Topic Areas
OpeningState Position Clearly (10 sec)

My view on this is fairly clear: I [strongly believe / am quite convinced] that [your position statement]. While I understand that this is a topic on which people disagree, I think the evidence and reasoning support my position quite compellingly.

Alternative Openers
This is an issue I feel strongly about. In my view, [position], and here's why I hold that perspective.
When I consider all the factors involved, I come down firmly on the side of [position], for several reasons.
MiddleDevelop 2–3 Arguments + Acknowledge Counterpoint (60–65 sec)

To begin with, [Argument #1 with concrete reasoning]. This matters because [real-world consequence or implication]. We can see evidence of this in [a relatable example — everyday life, a trend, a comparison with other contexts].

Beyond that, it's worth considering [Argument #2 — ideally addressing a different dimension: social, economic, ethical, or practical]. Research and common experience both suggest that [supporting observation or example], which reinforces my position considerably.

Now, I'm aware that some people argue [the strongest counterargument], and I think that's a legitimate concern. However, I'd respond to that by pointing out that [your rebuttal]. When you factor that in, the counterargument loses much of its force.

Opinion & Reasoning Connectors
The reason I hold this view is... / What informs my perspective here is...
Consider, for example, the fact that... / A clear illustration of this is...
While I understand the opposing view, I'd argue that... / That said, the counterpoint overlooks...
ClosingRestate + Broaden (15 sec)

Ultimately, I believe that [restatement of position in different words]. The decisions we make about [topic] now will have lasting consequences for [future generations / our communities / the quality of our daily lives], and that's precisely why I think this issue deserves serious and thoughtful consideration.

Alternative Closings
For all these reasons, I remain convinced that [position]. It's not the most popular view, perhaps, but I think it's the right one.
In the final analysis, the evidence points in one direction — and that's toward [position].
Opening

When it comes to [environmental or lifestyle topic], I take a fairly strong position: I believe that [your stance]. And I say that not as an abstract ideal, but as a practical necessity.

Middle

First, the evidence around [topic — climate impact / public health / urban sustainability] is increasingly difficult to ignore. [Specific observation or trend] demonstrates this more vividly than almost any other example I can think of.

From a personal and community standpoint, [Argument #2 — how this affects everyday choices or quality of life]. Many people underestimate [the cumulative impact / how accessible small changes can be / how much individual choices matter at scale].

I do recognize that [counterpoint — cost, inconvenience, systemic barriers] makes this challenging for some people. But I think [your rebuttal — collective action, policy support, gradual change] is the more productive and realistic framing.

Closing

Task 8 — Describing an Unusual Situation

Look at a surprising or unusual image and describe what's happening, why it might be happening, and how you would feel or react if you were there.

⏱ Prep: 30 sec 🎙 Speak: 60 sec
◆ CLB 10+ Strategy: Go beyond surface description — speculate about the backstory, explain the unusual element, and inject personality through your imagined reaction.
